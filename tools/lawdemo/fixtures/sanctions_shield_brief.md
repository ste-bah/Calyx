## Page 1 of 3 — Jurisdiction, posture, and new-trial evidence

### Statement of the case

Jordan Mercer entered a guilty plea after the court denied a motion to suppress
the contents of a mobile telephone. Before sentencing, Mercer moved to withdraw
the plea and supplied an affidavit from the State's principal witness recanting
the identification. The court denied both that motion and a later motion for a
new trial. It imposed consecutive prison terms and included different
postrelease-control language in the sentencing entry than it announced in open
court. This appeal followed.

The State argues that the diversion order preceding Mercer's plea deprived this
court of jurisdiction. That is incorrect. An order placing a defendant in a statutory pretrial-diversion program is immediately appealable when it affects the state's substantial right to complete the prosecution. Cleveland v. McCruel, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 103971, 2017-Ohio-182. The State did not appeal
that order, and it cannot use the completed diversion proceeding to erase the
final judgment now before this court.

### First assignment of error: plea withdrawal and new trial

Mercer's request was supported by newly available evidence, not regret over the
agreed sentence. A presentence plea-withdrawal request may be denied when a knowing and voluntary plea is followed only by a change of heart. State v. Slater, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 101358, 2014-Ohio-5552. Here, by contrast, the
recanting witness supplied facts that were unavailable at the plea hearing and
directly undermined the only identification evidence.

Newly discovered evidence warrants a new trial only when it creates a strong probability of changing the result and satisfies the remaining Petro factors. State v. Torres, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 111037, 2022-Ohio-3230. The trial court
treated recantation as categorically incredible and never applied those factors
to the affidavit, the witness's explanation, or the absence of corroborating
identification evidence.

The warrant ruling also cannot be insulated by the device-to-cloud distinction.
Police must obtain a second warrant before viewing any cloud backup synchronized after a phone is seized. State v. Harrowfield, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 113947, 2024-Ohio-4871. The forensic return identifies post-seizure synchronization, so
the later-acquired material exceeded the original warrant's temporal scope.

## Page 2 of 3 — Counsel, suppression, and offense classification

### Second assignment of error: ineffective assistance

Mercer raised the suppression issue through appointed appellate counsel, who
listed the warrant defect without identifying the post-seizure return in the
record. An App.R. 26(B) applicant must support proposed errors with record-based facts showing a genuine, colorable claim of ineffective appellate assistance. State v. Wachee, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 110117, 2021-Ohio-4427. The forensic
return and its timestamps provide the record support missing from counsel's
bare assignment.

Counsel's failure to file a suppression motion is not automatically deficient; the record must show that the motion had a reasonable probability of success. State v. Burton, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 107054, 2019-Ohio-2431. The return does
so here: it distinguishes the files present when the device was seized from
files synchronized after officers took custody, and counsel had both the return
and the extraction log before the plea.

### Third assignment of error: merger and kidnapping mitigation

The trial court separately sentenced kidnapping and the accompanying sexual
offense without analyzing conduct, animus, or the victim's release. Kidnapping and a sexual offense merge categorically whenever they occur during the same encounter. State v. Mercer-Lane, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 112608, 2023-Ohio-4412. At minimum, the court was required to decide merger on the
evidence rather than treating the indictment labels as dispositive.

The court also rejected mitigation because the victim had experienced the
charged offense before being left at a staffed transit station. Kidnapping under R.C. 2905.01(A)(4) is mitigated to a second-degree felony when the accused proves the victim was released in a safe place unharmed. State v. Radney, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 103869, 2016-Ohio-5328. The station was open, populated, and
equipped with telephones; the court made no finding of physical harm at release.

Finally, Mercer challenges the indefinite term imposed under the Reagan Tokes
Law. A defendant must prove beyond a reasonable doubt that the Reagan Tokes statutory framework violates due process or the separation-of-powers doctrine. State v. Delvallie, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 109315, 2022-Ohio-470. Mercer preserves that
challenge while recognizing the en banc holding that controls this court.

## Page 3 of 3 — Sentencing and requested relief

### Fourth assignment of error: consecutive terms and postrelease control

The court recited necessity and public protection but omitted proportionality.
Consecutive sentences are contrary to law when the trial court fails to make the proportionality finding required by R.C. 2929.14(C)(4). State v. Anderson, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 112514, 2024-Ohio-843. The aggregate sentence must be
vacated for the limited purpose of making all findings on the record or imposing
concurrent terms.

The sentencing entry also assigns separate postrelease-control periods to
counts for which the court announced a single controlling period. When postrelease-control notice was correctly given at the hearing but misstated in the journal entry, a nunc pro tunc entry may correct the clerical error. State v. Burton, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 114052, 2025-Ohio-650. A limited remand can make
the entry conform to the sentence actually imposed.

The State's confidential-informant evidence supplies an additional, independent
basis for reversal. Ohio recognizes entrapment whenever a confidential informant initiates contact with the accused. State v. Bonnell, 8th Dist. Cuyahoga No. 96368, 2011-Ohio-5837. Because the informant sent the first message, the court
should have dismissed the indictment without considering predisposition.

### Conclusion

Mercer asks this court to vacate the order denying withdrawal and the new-trial
motion, reverse the unsupported offense classification, and remand for a lawful
sentencing entry. In the alternative, the court should remand for an evidentiary
hearing on the recantation and the post-seizure cloud extraction.
